Mini Biography: Dr. Ruth Pfau

Dr. Ruth Katherina Martha Pfau was a German-born Pakistani physician, nun, and humanitarian who dedicated over 55 years of her life to eradicating leprosy in Pakistan. Widely revered as "Pakistan's Mother Teresa"

Early Life

Born in Germany in 1929, Dr. Ruth Pfau's early life was deeply impacted by the horrors of World War II and her family's daring escape from Soviet occupation. These traumatic events inspired her to study medicine, wishing to dedicate her life to healing others.

Dr. Ruth Pfau's Greatest Achievements

Dr. Ruth Pfau's most monumental achievement was leading a relentless, decades-long national campaign that successfully controlled Leprosy in Pakistan, making it the country in Asia to achieve this milestone in 1996, according to the World Health Organization (WHO).

She established the Marie Adelaide Leprosy Centre (MALC) in Karachi and expanded it into a massive network of over 150 leprosy clinics across all provinces of the country, transforming Pakistan from a nation heavily burdened by the disease into a global model for public health.

Correction Corner

Initially Dr. Ruth Pfau didn't practice in infectious diseases and Leprosy but practiced in gynecology and obstetrics.

Interesting Example

A Quiet Rebellion Against Fear

Early in her work in Karachi, Dr. Pfau noticed that even her own staff would hesitate before touching leprosy patients. They would wear gloves and masks, wash their hands frequently. They meant no harm by it, but it was a reflection of the fear that permeated the medical community at the time.

So she made a small, deliberate change.

When greeting a patient, she would deliberately reach out with both hands, clasp theirs warmly, and hold on longer than necessary.

"I wanted them to feel that I was not counting the seconds until I could let go. I wanted them to feel that I was not afraid of them and that I cared for them as human beings, not just as patients."

Words That Inspire

"You cannot change the whole world. But you can change a small part of it for one person."
